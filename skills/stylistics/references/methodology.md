# Stylistic Analysis Methodology

## I. Foundational Principles

This approach to analyzing writing style is governed by the following core principles:

1. **Rigorous:** The analysis must be grounded in explicit, structured models of language and discourse. It should move beyond subjective impressions to systematic observation based on defined linguistic concepts.

2. **Retrievable:** The analysis must employ clear terminology and criteria. The process of moving from description to interpretation should be transparent, allowing others to understand the analytical path taken.

3. **Replicable:** The methods should be sufficiently clear that the analysis could, in principle, be verified or applied to other texts by different analysts.

The fundamental premise is the **primacy of language**: The various forms, patterns, and levels constituting the text's linguistic structure are the primary data and the basis for exploring its function, effect, and interpretation.

---

## II. Levels of Language

Stylistic analysis involves a systematic examination of the text across seven interconnected levels of linguistic organization, arranged hierarchically but constantly interacting with one another.

---

### Level 1: Graphology (Written Representation)

Features of the text as a visual artifact.

**Elements to examine:**
- Spelling (standard, variant, deviant)
- Punctuation patterns and density
- Capitalization choices
- Font, typeface, and emphasis markers (bold, italic, underline)
- Text layout and spatial organization
- Paragraphing patterns and length
- Use of white space
- Lists, bullet points, numbering systems
- Tables, diagrams, visual elements

**Key questions:**
- How is the text organized visually on the page?
- Are there deviations from standard written conventions? If so, what function do they serve?
- How does graphological organization interact with grammatical structure?
- Does the visual layout create any additional layer of meaning?

---

### Level 2: Phonology (Sound Patterns)

Aspects related to the sound of language, even in written text.

**Elements to examine:**
- Rhythm and metre (patterns of stressed/unstressed syllables)
- Alliteration (consonant repetition)
- Assonance (vowel repetition)
- Rhyme (end rhyme, internal rhyme)
- Onomatopoeia and sound symbolism
- Pace and tempo implied by sentence structure

**Key questions:**
- Is there an identifiable rhythmic pattern? Is it regular or varied?
- Are there notable repetitions of sounds? What effect do they create?
- Does the sound patterning reinforce, or cut against, the meaning?
- How does sound patterning interact with grammatical boundaries?

---

### Level 3: Morphology (Word Structure)

The internal construction of words.

**Elements to examine:**
- Root words and their origins (Germanic, Latinate, Greek, etc.)
- Prefixes and their functions (negation, intensification, etc.)
- Suffixes and their functions (nominalization, adjectivalization, etc.)
- Compounding (creating new words by combining existing ones)
- Conversion (using words in unexpected grammatical classes)
- Neologisms (newly coined words)
- Abbreviations and acronyms

**Key questions:**
- Are words predominantly simple or complex in structure?
- Is there significant use of affixation? What types predominate?
- Are there any unusual word-formations or neologisms?
- What is the balance of Germanic vs. Latinate vocabulary? What register does this suggest?

---

### Level 4: Lexis (Vocabulary and Word Choice)

The selection of specific words.

**Elements to examine:**
- Denotation (literal meaning)
- Connotation (associative meaning, emotional coloring)
- Semantic fields (clusters of related words)
- Register and formality level
- Specificity vs. generality
- Concrete vs. abstract vocabulary
- Technical/specialized terminology
- Collocation patterns (words that habitually co-occur)
- Lexical repetition and variation

**Key questions:**
- What semantic fields dominate the text? What does this reveal about its concerns?
- What is the overall register? Formal, informal, technical, colloquial?
- Are there shifts in register? Where and why?
- Is the vocabulary predominantly abstract or concrete?
- Are there unusual collocations or word choices? What effect do they create?
- What connotations attach to key lexical choices?

**Register — Field dimension:**
The **field** (subject matter) determines the vocabulary domain. Examine what specialized vocabulary the text draws upon and how it signals its disciplinary or topical territory. Register shifts in vocabulary often mark transitions between topics or modes of argument.

---

### Level 5: Grammar and Syntax

The arrangement of words into phrases, clauses, and sentences.

#### 5.1 Clause Structure

**Elements to examine:**
- Clause elements: Subject (S), Predicator (P), Complement (C), Adjunct (A)
- Typical clause patterns (SPC, SPCA, SPA, etc.)
- Position and weight of each element
- Anticipatory constituents (elements placed before the main clause)
- Trailing constituents (elements placed after the main clause)
- Minor clauses (structures lacking a full predicator)
- Ellipsis (grammatical abbreviation)

**Key questions:**
- What is the typical clause pattern in the text?
- Are clause elements arranged in canonical order, or are there significant re-orderings?
- Are there "heavy" subjects or complements? What effect does this create?
- Where are adjuncts typically positioned? What information do they add?
- Is ellipsis used? In what contexts?

**Analytical technique - Tag test:**
Adding a tag question (e.g., "doesn't she?", "will they?") helps identify the Subject (returned as pronoun) and aspects of the Predicator (returned as auxiliary).

#### 5.2 Sentence Types and Complexity

**Elements to examine:**
- Sentence types by function: declarative, interrogative, imperative, exclamative
- Sentence complexity: simple (one clause), compound (coordinated clauses), complex (subordinated clauses)
- Coordination patterns (and, but, or)
- Subordination patterns (because, although, when, if, etc.)
- Embedding depth (clauses within clauses)
- Sentence length and variation

**Key questions:**
- What sentence types predominate? What does this suggest about the text's purpose?
- What is the balance of simple, compound, and complex sentences?
- Is coordination or subordination more prevalent?
- How deeply embedded are subordinate clauses?
- Is there significant variation in sentence length, or is it consistent?

#### 5.3 Grammatical Functions

**Elements to examine:**
- Tense choices (past, present, future) and their functions
- Aspect (simple, progressive, perfect) and its functions
- Voice (active vs. passive) and its distribution
- Modality (modal verbs expressing possibility, necessity, obligation, permission, etc.)
- Mood (indicative, subjunctive, conditional)
- Negation patterns

**Key questions:**
- What tenses predominate? Why?
- Is there significant use of progressive or perfect aspect? What effect?
- What is the ratio of active to passive voice? In what contexts is passive used?
- What modal verbs appear? What degrees of certainty, obligation, or possibility do they express?
- Are there patterns of negation? What is being denied or excluded?

#### 5.4 Noun Phrases and Modification

**Elements to examine:**
- Head nouns and their types
- Pre-modification (determiners, adjectives, nouns as modifiers)
- Post-modification (prepositional phrases, relative clauses, non-finite clauses)
- Noun phrase complexity and length
- Nominalization (turning verbs/adjectives into nouns)

**Key questions:**
- How complex are noun phrases? Heavily modified or simple?
- Is pre-modification or post-modification more common?
- Are there patterns in adjective use? What attributes are emphasized?
- Is there significant nominalization? What processes or qualities are being "thing-ified"?

#### 5.5 Verb Phrases

**Elements to examine:**
- Lexical verb choices (action, state, process)
- Auxiliary verb patterns
- Finite vs. non-finite verb forms
- Verb phrase complexity

**Key questions:**
- What types of verbs predominate (action, mental, relational)?
- Are verb phrases simple or complex?
- Is there significant use of non-finite forms (infinitives, participles)?

#### 5.6 Transitivity

Transitivity analysis examines how experience is represented linguistically — who does what to whom, in what circumstances.

**Process Types:**

| Process Type | Meaning | Key Verb Examples | Participants |
|--------------|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Material** | Actions, events, doings | run, build, break, give | Actor, Goal, Beneficiary |
| **Mental** | Perception, cognition, affection | see, think, want, believe | Sensor, Phenomenon |
| **Relational** | Being, having, attributes | be, have, seem, become | Carrier/Attribute or Identified/Identifier |
| **Behavioural** | Physiological/psychological behaviour | breathe, laugh, dream, stare | Behaver |
| **Verbal** | Saying | say, tell, ask, report | Sayer, Receiver, Verbiage |
| **Existential** | Existing | there is, exist, arise | Existent |

**Participant Roles:**
- **Actor:** The doer of a material process
- **Goal:** The entity affected by a material process
- **Sensor:** The experiencer of a mental process
- **Phenomenon:** What is sensed in a mental process
- **Sayer:** The speaker in a verbal process
- **Carrier:** The entity bearing an attribute in a relational process
- **Attribute:** The quality ascribed to a carrier

**Circumstances** provide additional information about where, when, how, and why processes occur: Temporal (when?), Spatial/Locative (where?), Manner (how?), Cause (why?), Accompaniment (with whom?), Role (as what?).

**Key questions:**
- What process types predominate? What does this suggest about how experience is being construed?
- Who or what occupies Actor positions? Who is represented as doing things?
- Who or what occupies Goal positions? Who is represented as affected by actions?
- Are there patterns of agency? Is agency clear or obscured?
- Is there **meronymic agency** (body parts as actors: "his hand reached") vs. **holonymic agency** (whole persons as actors)?
- How is passive voice used? What agents are deleted or backgrounded?
- What circumstances are specified? What is left unspecified?

#### 5.7 Speech and Thought Representation (Grammatical Aspects)

**Categories of speech representation:**

| Category | Example | Features |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **Direct Speech (DS)** | She said, "I will come tomorrow." | Quotation marks; original pronouns, tense, deixis |
| **Indirect Speech (IS)** | She said that she would come the next day. | "that" clause; shifted pronouns, tense, deixis |
| **Free Direct Speech (FDS)** | I will come tomorrow. | No reporting clause; original forms |
| **Free Indirect Speech (FIS)** | She would come the next day. | No reporting clause; shifted forms |
| **Narrative Report of Speech (NRS)** | She spoke about her plans. | Summary; no indication of actual words |

**Conversion rules (Direct → Indirect):**
1. Remove quotation marks; add "that" clause
2. Shift pronouns (1st/2nd → 3rd person)
3. Shift proximal deixis to distal (here→there, this→that, now→then, tomorrow→the next day)
4. Backshift tenses (present→past, will→would, etc.)
5. Change direction of movement verbs (come→go)

Thought representation follows parallel categories: Direct Thought (DT), Indirect Thought (IT), Free Direct Thought (FDT), Free Indirect Thought (FIT), Narrative Report of Thought (NRT).

---

### Level 6: Semantics (Meaning)

The meanings conveyed by words, phrases, and sentences.

**Elements to examine:**
- Denotative (literal) meaning
- Connotative (associative) meaning
- Ambiguity (multiple possible meanings)
- Semantic relations: synonymy, antonymy, hyponymy, meronymy
- Semantic prosody (consistent positive/negative associations of a word)
- Presupposition (what the text assumes to be true)
- Entailment (what necessarily follows from statements)

**Key questions:**
- Are there key terms that carry special meaning in this text?
- Is there ambiguity? Is it deliberate or accidental?
- What oppositions or contrasts are established?
- What is presupposed rather than stated?
- Are there semantic tensions or contradictions?

---

### Level 7: Pragmatics and Discourse

How language functions in context beyond the sentence level.

#### 7.1 Speech Acts and Illocutionary Force

**Elements to examine:**
- Speech act types: assertives (stating), directives (commanding/requesting), commissives (promising), expressives (expressing feelings), declaratives (effecting change)
- Direct vs. indirect speech acts
- Illocutionary force indicators

**Key questions:**
- What is the text doing with language? Asserting? Persuading? Instructing?
- Are speech acts performed directly or indirectly?
- What is the relationship between grammatical form and communicative function?

#### 7.2 Implicature and Inference

**Elements to examine:**
- What is said vs. what is implicated
- Conversational maxims (quantity, quality, relation, manner) and their exploitation
- Conventional implicatures (tied to specific words like "but", "even", "yet")

**Key questions:**
- What meanings are implied rather than stated?
- Are conversational maxims followed or flouted? For what effect?
- What must the reader infer to make sense of the text?

#### 7.3 Cohesion

**Elements to examine:**
- Reference (pronouns, demonstratives, the article "the")
- Substitution and ellipsis
- Conjunction and logical connectors
- Lexical cohesion (repetition, synonymy, collocation)

**Key questions:**
- How is the text held together linguistically?
- What cohesive devices predominate?
- Are there gaps in cohesion? What effect do they have?
- How are topics maintained and developed across the text?

#### 7.4 Information Structure

**Elements to examine:**
- Given vs. new information
- Theme (sentence opening) and rheme (remainder)
- Focus and emphasis
- Foregrounding and backgrounding

**Key questions:**
- What information is presented as given? As new?
- What typically appears in theme position? What does this foreground?
- How is emphasis achieved?

#### 7.5 Point of View and Perspective

**Planes of point of view:**

| Plane | Focus | Key Questions |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| **Spatial** | Physical position | Where is the viewing position? What can be seen from here? |
| **Temporal** | Time position | When is the narrating occurring relative to events? Retrospective? Concurrent? |
| **Psychological** | Mental access | Whose thoughts/perceptions are represented? Internal or external focalization? |
| **Ideological** | Values and beliefs | What value system is the text mediated through? Whose worldview predominates? |

**Focalization:**
- **Internal focalization:** Events filtered through a character's consciousness
- **External focalization:** No access to any character's thoughts; purely behavioral description
- **Zero focalization:** Omniscient; access to multiple characters' minds

**Deictic markers** — language that "points" and requires context for interpretation:
- **Spatial deixis:** here/there, this/that, come/go
- **Temporal deixis:** now/then, today/yesterday, present/past tense
- **Personal deixis:** I/you/he/she/they

Key questions: Where is the deictic centre? Are there shifts in deictic centre? How do deictic choices position the reader?

**Locative expressions** indicate location, direction, and physical setting: prepositional phrases of place, adverbs of direction, verbs of motion and direction.

#### 7.6 Speech and Thought Representation (Pragmatic Aspects)

**Key questions:**
- What modes of speech/thought representation are used?
- How much of the original words are preserved?
- What is the effect of the chosen mode? Distance? Intimacy? Irony?
- Are there shifts between modes? What triggers them?

#### 7.7 Register

Register refers to language variation according to use (context).

**Components:**
- **Field:** What is being talked about; subject matter (connects to Level 4 vocabulary choices)
- **Tenor:** The relationship between participants; formality, power relations
- **Mode:** The channel of communication; spoken, written, mixed

**Key questions:**
- What field is indicated by vocabulary and content?
- What tenor is indicated by formality level, pronoun choices, politeness markers?
- What mode is indicated? Does the text show features of spoken or written language, or both?
- Are there register shifts? What triggers them and what effect do they have?

#### 7.8 Dialect

Dialect refers to language variation according to user (region, social group).

**Types:**
- Regional dialect
- Social dialect (sociolect)
- Temporal dialect (historical language)
- Idiolect (individual speaker's language)

**Key questions:**
- Are there non-standard dialect features? What variety do they represent?
- What is the function of dialect representation? Authenticity? Characterization? Humor?
- What attitudes toward the dialect/speaker might be implied?

## III. Foregrounding: A Cross-Cutting Principle

Foregrounding is the general term for making a feature stand out. It operates **across all seven levels** and is identified by comparing patterns against norms — either the text's own internal norms or external linguistic norms.

### Types of Foregrounding

- **Deviation:** Departing from expected patterns or norms
- **Parallelism:** Unexpected regularity; repetition of patterns

### Deviation by Level

| Level | Type of Deviation | Examples |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| Level 1: Graphology | Unusual spelling, punctuation, layout | Missing punctuation, unconventional capitalization |
| Level 2: Phonology | Unusual sound patterns | Broken rhythm, unexpected alliteration |
| Level 3: Morphology | Unusual word-formation | Neologisms, unexpected affixation |
| Level 4: Lexis | Unusual word choices | Archaisms, neologisms, register violations |
| Level 5: Grammar | Unusual structures | Inverted syntax, fragmented sentences |
| Level 6: Semantics | Unusual meanings | Metaphor, paradox, contradiction |
| Level 7: Pragmatics | Unusual speech acts, register violations | Irony, sarcasm, flouted maxims |

### Key Questions

- What patterns can be identified as the text's norms?
- What elements deviate from these norms? What elements show unexpected parallelism?
- What is foregrounded? What effect does this foregrounding achieve?
- Is the deviation internal (against the text's own norms) or external (against general linguistic norms)?

## IV. Analytical Procedure

### Step 1: Initial Reading
Read the text through without analysis. Note your initial impressions and intuitions. What strikes you? What seems unusual or significant?

### Step 2: Identify Context
- What is the text type/genre?
- What is the apparent purpose?
- Who is the apparent author? Audience?
- What is the medium and mode?

### Step 3: Systematic Analysis by Level
Work through each of the seven levels systematically. Each level already contains its detailed guidance (including transitivity, speech representation, point of view, and register, now integrated into the relevant levels). At each level, identify patterns and note significant features.

### Step 4: Identify Foregrounded Features
Review findings across all levels for deviation and parallelism (see Section III). Identify what is foregrounded and to what effect.

### Step 5: Integration and Interpretation
- How do features at different levels interact?
- What stylistic effects are created?
- How does form relate to meaning and function?
- What interpretation does the linguistic evidence support?

### Step 6: Synthesis
- What is the overall stylistic profile of the text?
- What are its distinctive features?
- How does it compare with other texts of its type?

### Step 7: Generate Style Guide
Transform the descriptive analytical findings into a prescriptive style guide following the same 7-level structure (see Section V).

## V. From Analysis to Style Guide

The ultimate purpose of stylistic analysis in this context is to produce an **actionable style guide** that can direct future writing. This section specifies how to transform descriptive observations into prescriptive writing rules.

### 5.1 Purpose and Principles

A style guide derived from analysis must be:

1. **Prescriptive:** Use imperative language ("Use...", "Avoid...", "Prefer...") rather than descriptive language ("The text uses...", "There is a tendency toward...")

2. **Specific:** Provide concrete guidance with exact words, structures, and patterns — not vague generalizations

3. **Exemplified:** Include authentic examples from the analyzed corpus for every rule

4. **Executable:** Written so that a writer (human or AI) can follow the rules without ambiguity

5. **Complete:** Cover all seven levels of language with sufficient detail to reproduce the style

### 5.2 Style Guide Structure

The style guide Part II follows the same seven levels used in the analysis. At each level, provide prescriptive rules with authentic examples from the analyzed text.

#### Level 1: Graphology

Specify rules for visual and formatting conventions:
- Typography: headers, fonts, emphasis markers
- Punctuation: conventions, density, special uses
- Layout: paragraphing, white space, visual organization
- Visual elements: lists, tables, numbering systems

Example formulation: "Use [punctuation pattern] for [purpose]. Format headers as [convention]. Avoid [visual pattern]."

#### Level 2: Phonology

Specify rules for rhythm and sound patterns (may be sparse for non-literary prose):
- Rhythm and tempo through sentence structure
- Alliteration, assonance, and other sound devices
- When sound patterning reinforces meaning

Example formulation: "Vary sentence length to create [rhythm effect]. Use alliteration in [contexts]."

#### Level 3: Morphology

Specify rules for word formation preferences:
- Germanic vs. Latinate vocabulary preference
- Nominalization: degree and contexts
- Affixation patterns
- Abbreviation and acronym conventions

Example formulation: "Prefer [Germanic/Latinate] forms for [contexts]. Use nominalization [frequently/moderately/sparingly]. Nominalize [verb/adjective] as [form]."

#### Level 4: Lexis

Specify rules for vocabulary and word choice:
- Register and formality level
- Semantic fields and their key vocabulary (with preferred/avoided terms)
- Technical terminology (usage, definition conventions)
- Collocation patterns
- Concrete vs. abstract vocabulary balance

Example formulation: "Maintain [formality level]. Use [semantic field] vocabulary such as [terms]. Prefer [term A] over [term B] for [concept]. Collocate [word] with [approved collocates]."

#### Level 5: Grammar and Syntax

Specify rules for grammatical and syntactic patterns:
- Sentence types: distribution targets (declarative/interrogative/imperative)
- Sentence complexity: target length, simple/compound/complex ratio, maximum embedding
- Sentence openings: approved theme-position patterns
- Coordination and subordination: preferred conjunctions and logical connectors
- Voice: active/passive ratio and contexts
- Tense, aspect, and modality: patterns and targets
- Transitivity: preferred process types, agency patterns, participant roles
- Speech and thought representation: grammatical conventions for citing others

Example formulation: "Target sentence length of [X–Y] words. Use passive voice for [contexts]. Express agency through [pattern]. Use [modal verbs] with [ratio]."

#### Level 6: Semantics

Specify rules for meaning patterns:
- Denotative and connotative preferences
- Presupposition patterns (what to assume vs. state)
- Figurative language: when and how to use metaphor, metonymy, etc.
- Semantic relations: preferred oppositions, synonymy patterns
- Semantic prosody: positive/negative associations of key terms

Example formulation: "Use [term] with its [connotation]. Presuppose [what]. Use metaphor for [contexts]. Avoid [semantic pattern]."

#### Level 7: Pragmatics and Discourse

Specify rules for discourse-level behaviour:
- Voice, stance, and persona: who the implied author is, what authority they claim
- Reader relationship: pronoun patterns, how the reader is positioned
- Epistemic stance: hedging/boosting balance, certainty calibration
- Evaluative stance: how explicitly evaluative, approved evaluative vocabulary
- Cohesion: preferred discourse markers and transition patterns
- Information structure: given-new progression, theme preferences
- Citation and attribution: density, style, reporting verbs, quotation conventions
- Document structure: required sections, heading conventions, introduction/conclusion patterns
- Point of view: focalization, deictic patterns, perspective management
- Argumentation: claim-evidence-warrant patterns, counterargument handling
- Register: field/tenor/mode conventions, register shift patterns

Example formulation: "Write as [persona]. Address the reader as [relationship]. Hedge claims with [terms] in [contexts]. Cite using [format]. Structure arguments as [pattern]."

### 5.3 Transformation Rules

When converting analytical observations to prescriptive rules, apply these transformations:

| Analytical Observation | Prescriptive Rule Format |
|----------------------|-------------------------|
| "X occurs frequently" | "Use X in [contexts]" |
| "X is rare" | "Avoid X except in [contexts]" |
| "X and Y both occur" | "Prefer X for [context A], Y for [context B]" |
| "X occurs in ratio A:B with Y" | "Use X approximately [A/(A+B)]% of the time" |
| "X occurs at position P" | "Place X at [position P]" |
| "X co-occurs with Y" | "When using X, pair with Y" |

### 5.4 Completeness Checklist

Before finalizing a style guide, verify it addresses all seven levels:

- [ ] Level 1: Typography, punctuation, layout conventions
- [ ] Level 2: Rhythm, tempo, sound patterns
- [ ] Level 3: Word formation, nominalization preferences
- [ ] Level 4: Register, semantic fields, terminology, collocations
- [ ] Level 5: Sentence patterns, transitivity, voice, modality
- [ ] Level 6: Meaning patterns, presupposition, figurative language
- [ ] Level 7: Stance, cohesion, citation, document structure, point of view

### 5.5 Example Transformation

**Analytical finding:**
> Modal verbs appear in the following frequencies: can (1,220), will (990), would (673), should (499), must (443), may (369). Hedging modals (may, might) are relatively infrequent compared to deontic modals (must, should).

**Transformed to prescriptive rule (Level 5: Grammar and Syntax):**

> **Modality and Certainty**
>
> Express claims with confidence. Use strong modals to convey necessity and obligation:
> - Use "must" for logical necessity: "This must be understood as..."
> - Use "should" for recommendations: "Analysis should account for..."
> - Use "can" for possibility and capability: "This framework can explain..."
> - Use "will" for predictions and intentions: "This article will demonstrate..."
>
> Limit tentative modals:
> - Use "may" sparingly, only for genuine uncertainty
> - Avoid "might" except for remote possibilities
>
> Target ratio: Strong modals (must, should, will, can) should outnumber weak modals (may, might) by at least 4:1.

## VI. Key Terminology Quick Reference

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Foregrounding** | Making a feature stand out through deviation or parallelism |
| **Transitivity** | The system for representing experience through process types and participants |
| **Focalization** | The perspective through which events are perceived |
| **Deixis** | Words requiring context to interpret (I, here, now, this) |
| **Modality** | Expression of possibility, necessity, obligation, permission |
| **Register** | Language variety according to use (field, tenor, mode) |
| **Collocation** | Habitual co-occurrence of words |
| **Cohesion** | Linguistic ties that hold a text together |
| **Speech act** | What an utterance does (assert, command, promise, etc.) |
| **Nominalization** | Converting verbs or adjectives into nouns |
